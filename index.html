How the globe is shaded

Shia (Ja'fari) Prayer Windows

Each pixel on the globe is colored by computing the sun's altitude angle at that location in real time, then classifying which of the five prayer windows applies. The Ja'fari (Shia Ithna-Ashari) method uses the following astronomical definitions:

Fajr
Begins when the sun is 16° below the horizon (on the rising / morning side). This marks the start of astronomical dawn. Fajr lasts until sunrise (sun crosses 0°).
Dhuhr
Begins at solar noon (sun crosses the observer's meridian at its highest daily altitude) and lasts until Asr.
Asr
Begins when the shadow of an object equals its own length plus the shadow at noon (shadow factor = 1, also used by the Shafi'i school). Mathematically: alt_asr = arctan(1 / (1 + tan(|lat − decl|))). Asr lasts until sunset.
Maghrib
Unlike most Sunni methods which start Maghrib at sunset (0°), the Ja'fari method waits until the sun is 4° below the horizon on the setting side. This accounts for the "redness of the eastern sky" fading. Maghrib lasts until Isha.
Isha
Begins when the sun is 14° below the horizon on the setting side. Isha extends through the night until the next day's Fajr.

Day / Night Shading

The base globe blends between a daytime satellite texture (NASA Blue Marble) and a nighttime city-lights texture, using the sun's altitude at each pixel. The transition is gradual across the twilight zone. The warm glow at the terminator (sunrise/sunset line) simulates atmospheric scattering.

High Latitudes (Aqrab al-Bilād)

Above 60° N/S the standard altitude-based definitions break down: at the summer solstice the sun never falls 16° below the horizon (no astronomical Fajr), and above the Arctic / Antarctic Circle it may never rise or set at all. Classical fiqh did not address these regions, so contemporary Marāji‘ (Sistani, Khamenei, Sādiq al-Shīrāzī) give the dominant ruling as Aqrab al-Bilād — "the nearest locality" — meaning to follow the schedule of the closest latitude where the regular calculation works.

Aqrab al-Bilād gives one location one schedule, not a spatial coloring — projecting it per-pixel onto a polar cap produces a kaleidoscope of every prayer state happening at 60° latitude around the world, which isn't visually meaningful. So above 60° the prayer overlay fades out and the polar caps just show geography. If you tap a high-latitude location, the side panel still shows the schedule projected from 60° on the same longitude (with a "projected from 60°" note), since that's where Aqrab al-Bilād is genuinely useful. Other rulings exist (middle-of-night, sevenths, angle-based) and produce different schedules.

Calculation Details

The sun's position (declination + subsolar longitude) is computed from a simplified NOAA solar algorithm, accurate to ~0.01°. Prayer windows are classified per pixel in a GLSL fragment shader, so the visualization runs at full frame rate with smooth, continuous bands — not a discretized grid.

Prayer times in the side panel are computed by the Adhan.js library using the same Ja'fari parameters. Local time zones are resolved via tz-lookup.
