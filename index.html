How the globe is shaded

Shia (Ja'fari) Prayer Windows

Each pixel on the globe is colored by computing the sun's altitude angle at that location in real time, then classifying which of the five prayer windows applies. The Ja'fari (Shia Ithnā-Asharī) method uses the following astronomical definitions, each rooted in Qur'ānic verses and traditions narrated from the Imams (peace be upon them):

Fajr
Begins when the sun is 16° below the horizon on the rising side — the start of astronomical dawn (al-fajr al-ṣādiq, "true dawn"), distinguished from the brief vertical glow of al-fajr al-kādhib ("false dawn"). Fajr lasts until sunrise.

حَتَّىٰ يَتَبَيَّنَ لَكُمُ الْخَيْطُ الْأَبْيَضُ مِنَ الْخَيْطِ الْأَسْوَدِ مِنَ الْفَجْرِ"…until the white thread of dawn becomes distinguishable to you from the black thread of night…" — Qur'ān 2:187

وَقْتُ الْفَجْرِ حِينَ يَنْشَقُّ الْفَجْرُ إِلَىٰ أَنْ تَطْلُعَ الشَّمْسُ"The time of Fajr is from when the dawn breaks until the sun rises." — Imam Ja'far al-Ṣādiq, al-Kāfī III

Dhuhr
Begins at solar noon (zawāl): the sun crosses the observer's meridian at its highest daily altitude. Dhuhr's dedicated time (waqt) extends all the way to sunset, shared with Asr after shadow factor 1 — the globe shows Asr in that overlap because it's the later-starting (and therefore "current") prayer, but Dhuhr is still valid.

أَقِمِ الصَّلَاةَ لِدُلُوكِ الشَّمْسِ إِلَىٰ غَسَقِ اللَّيْلِ"Establish prayer at the decline of the sun until the darkness of the night." — Qur'ān 17:78

إِذَا زَالَتِ الشَّمْسُ دَخَلَ الْوَقْتَانِ: الظُّهْرُ وَالْعَصْرُ"When the sun crosses the meridian, both times enter: Ẓuhr and ‘Aṣr." — Imam Muḥammad al-Bāqir, al-Kāfī III — the textual basis for the Ja'fari "shared-time" ruling.

Asr
Its dedicated time begins when the shadow of an upright object equals its own length, taken from the shadow at noon (shadow factor = 1, matching the Shāfi‘ī school). Mathematically: alt_asr = arctan(1 / (1 + tan(|lat − decl|))). Asr lasts until sunset.

حَافِظُوا عَلَى الصَّلَوَاتِ وَالصَّلَاةِ الْوُسْطَىٰ"Guard strictly the prayers, and the middle prayer…" — traditionally identified as ‘Aṣr in Shia exegesis. — Qur'ān 2:238

إِذَا صَارَ ظِلُّ كُلِّ شَيْءٍ مِثْلَهُ فَقَدْ دَخَلَ وَقْتُ الْعَصْرِ"When the shadow of every thing has become equal to it, the time of ‘Aṣr has entered." — Imam al-Ṣādiq, Tahdhīb al-Aḥkām II

Maghrib
Unlike Sunni methods that start Maghrib at sunset (0°), the Ja'fari method waits until the sun is 4° below the horizon on the setting side — the moment the eastern redness (al-ḥumra al-mashriqiyya) has fully faded. Maghrib's dedicated time extends until shar'ī midnight (the midpoint between sunset and the next dawn at the Fajr angle), shared with Isha after the western twilight has disappeared. The globe computes that midpoint exactly per pixel from the closed-form sunset and dawn hour-angles, including the equation-of-time correction; it sits roughly 30 min before solar midnight at the equator (and varies with latitude and season). The globe shows Isha in that overlap as the current prayer.

وَأَقِمِ الصَّلَاةَ طَرَفَيِ النَّهَارِ وَزُلَفًا مِّنَ اللَّيْلِ"And establish prayer at the two ends of the day and at the approach of the night." — Qur'ān 11:114

إِذَا ذَهَبَتِ الْحُمْرَةُ مِنَ الْمَشْرِقِ فَقَدْ غَرَبَتِ الشَّمْسُ"When the redness has departed from the east, then the sun has [truly] set." — Imam al-Ṣādiq, al-Kāfī III — the explicit basis for the 4° offset.

Isha
Begins when the sun is 14° below the horizon on the setting side — the disappearance of the western twilight (shafaq). Isha's dedicated time (waqt) ends at shar'ī midnight (the midpoint between sunset and the next dawn at the Fajr angle), not at the next Fajr — after that point the prayer can be offered as qaḍā' (makeup) but is no longer in its dedicated time. The shader derives this midpoint per-pixel from the closed-form sunset and dawn hour-angles for each (latitude, declination), so the cutoff is exact — not the antisolar-meridian approximation — and equation-of-time is already baked into the sun direction.

أَقِمِ الصَّلَاةَ لِدُلُوكِ الشَّمْسِ إِلَىٰ غَسَقِ اللَّيْلِ"…until the darkness of the night (ghasaq al-layl)." — Qur'ān 17:78; Imam al-Ṣādiq glosses ghasaq al-layl as midnight in Tafsīr al-Qummī.

وَقْتُ الْعِشَاءِ حِينَ يَغِيبُ الشَّفَقُ إِلَىٰ نِصْفِ اللَّيْلِ"The time of Isha is from when the twilight (shafaq) has disappeared until midnight." — Imam al-Ṣādiq, al-Kāfī III

High Latitudes (Aqrab al-Bilād)

The altitude-based Ja'fari calculation breaks in two sun-relative ways: (a) where the sun never reaches Fajr's −16° below horizon — |φ + δ| > 74° — or (b) where the sun never rises above the horizon at all (polar night, |φ − δ| > 90°). The cap kicks in at whichever boundary you cross first. That makes it tilt with the season: in northern summer the Arctic cap is Fajr-driven (~51°N), while the Antarctic cap is polar-night-driven (~67°S, since the sun never rises there). Six months later the asymmetry flips. As you scrub the date, the caps follow the sun's declination. Classical fiqh did not address these regions, so contemporary Marāji‘ (Sistani, Khamenei, Sādiq al-Shīrāzī) give the dominant ruling as Aqrab al-Bilād — "the nearest locality" — meaning to follow the schedule of the closest latitude where the regular calculation works.

Aqrab al-Bilād gives one location one schedule, not a spatial coloring — projecting it per-pixel onto a polar cap produces a kaleidoscope of every prayer state happening at the threshold latitude around the world, which isn't visually meaningful. So inside the cap the prayer overlay fades out and the polar regions just show geography. If you tap a high-latitude location, the side panel still shows the schedule projected to the threshold latitude on the same longitude (with a "projected from N°" note), since that's where Aqrab al-Bilād is genuinely useful. Other rulings exist (middle-of-night, sevenths, angle-based) and produce different schedules.

Calculation Details

The sun's position (declination + subsolar longitude) is computed from a simplified NOAA solar algorithm, accurate to ~0.01°. Prayer windows are classified per pixel in a GLSL fragment shader, so the visualization runs at full frame rate with smooth, continuous bands — not a discretized grid.

Prayer times in the side panel are computed by the Adhan.js library using the same Ja'fari parameters. Local time zones are resolved via tz-lookup.
